About this Data Story

Raising Awareness for Threatened and Endangered Species within the Parks

Throughout the world, industrial development, habitat loss, and changing climate patterns due to human activities threaten the existence of much of the planet’s biodiversity.

In the United States, the National Park System provides a case study in protecting and preserving the critical habitats of endangered and threatened species.

As frequent visitors to our Nation’s Parks, our team grew interested in better understanding the threatened and endangered species that the parks are protecting.

Consequently, we developed this Data Story to shine a light on the species present within the nation’s most visited parks and to highlight opportunities for viewers to take action. As the Parks celebrate their 150th anniversary, we hope to support and celebrate the biodiversity within them for generations to come.

Understanding Visitation Numbers Over 20 Years

How do the parks rank (annually) by visitation numbers?

To continue our exploration of 2021's most visited National Parks, we investigated how popularity (i.e., visitation numbers) between the parks changed over the past 20 years.

Insight: Whereas the nine other parks have traded rankings over the past twenty years, Great Smoky Mountains National Parks has consistently ranked as the number one most visited park over this time. Upon further inquiry, we learned that Great Smoky Mountains is the only National Park that does not charge an entrance fee. Thus, visitors are attracted by the accessibility of this free park!

Hover over a park's trend line to view a highlighted timeline of the park's visitation ranking over the past 20 years.
