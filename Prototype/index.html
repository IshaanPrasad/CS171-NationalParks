About this Data Story

Raising Awareness for Threatened and Endangered Species within the Parks

Throughout the world, industrial development, habitat loss, and changing climate patterns due to human activities threaten the existence of much of the planet’s biodiversity.

In the United States, the National Park System provides a case study in protecting and preserving the critical habitats of endangered and threatened species.

As frequent visitors to our Nation’s Parks, our team grew interested in better understanding the threatened and endangered species that the parks are protecting.

Consequently, we developed this Data Story to raise awareness on the species present within the nation’s most visited parks and to highlight opportunities for viewers to take action. As the Parks celebrate their 150th anniversary, we hope to support and celebrate the biodiversity within them for generations to come.

How do the parks rank (annually) by visitation numbers?

To continue our exploration of 2021's most visited National Parks, we investigated how popularity (i.e., visitation numbers) between the parks changed over the past 20 years.

Insight: Whereas the nine other parks have traded rankings over the past twenty years, Great Smoky Mountains National Parks has consistently ranked as the number one most visited park over this time. Moving Forward

As humanity continues to work together to protect the biodiversity on our planet, the U.S. National Park System serves as an excellent example of an institution that has set the standard for the conservation and restoration of vulnerable populations. These protected areas provide safe havens for a wide range of plants and animals, allowing them to thrive and reproduce without the threat of human interference.

In addition to providing safe habitats, National Parks often employ a variety of strategies to restore and conserve vulnerable species. These can include habitat restoration, reintroduction programs, and conservation breeding efforts.

One of the most well-known examples of these purposeful reintroduction efforts is the Yellowstone Wolf Restoration Project. In the mid-1990s, wolves were reintroduced to Yellowstone National Park after being nearly extirpated from the area. This effort has been incredibly successful, with the wolf population now thriving and playing a key role in the park's ecosystem.

Ultimately, increased biodiversity and visitation numbers can work in a symbiotic cycle of positive reinforcement. In particular, many park visitors are often drawn to these protected regions to see a wide range of plants and animals, and National Parks with high levels of biodiversity are likely to be more popular with visitors.

In turn, highly visited National Parks can be extremely helpful for protecting vulnerable species. The increased attention and resources provided by high levels of visitation can help support conservation and restoration efforts, ensuring that these species continue to thrive and recover.

How to Help

Five Ways to Support National Parks and their Vulnerable Populations

  1. Visit the National Parks and support conservation efforts by paying the park entrance fee. This money helps fund the park's operations and conservation efforts.
  2. Volunteer conservation efforts by purchasing products made from sustainable materials and by practicing sustainable habits, such as reducing your carbon footprint and conserving water.
  3. Support your time at a national park. Many national parks offer volunteer opportunities that range from trail maintenance to animal monitoring.
  4. Educate yourself and others about the importance of national parks and their endangered species. Share information about the parks and their conservation efforts on social media and with friends and family. Sharing this Project can be a great first step!
  5. Donate organizations that support national parks and their conservation efforts, such as the National Park Foundation or the World Wildlife Fund.
